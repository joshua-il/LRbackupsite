  India is an ancient civilization rich with traditions and history dating back thousands of years. But since India's independence from England a half century ago, it has become the world's largest democracy governing one sixth of our planets population.

India's population sailed past the one billion mark sometime August the nineteenth, nineteen ninety-nine. At present projections India will exceed 1.5 billion people by two thousand forty-five, sliding into first place ahead of China.

Explosive growth presents obvious challenges in the areas of health, nutrition, education, public services, and employment, not to mention the environment. Seventy five percent of their poor and impoverished three hundred and fifty million struggle in some seven hundred thousand rural villages.
 
 


The records indicate that twelve and one half million of these children are officially in full-time employment, and the status of a remaining seventy four million is unknown. Most of the eighty seven million children that are not in school, work on family farms alongside their parents or are paid agricultural laborers and domestic servants. But child occupations include prostitution, soldiering and crime. Child Welfare Organizations estimate that there are five hundred thousand street children nationwide. 		 


Over 1 million women and young girls are active in the countries sex industry at any given time. Women's rights organizations and Non Government Organizations estimate that more than twelve thousand and perhaps as many as fifty thousand women and children are trafficked into India annually, from neighboring states, feed the insatiable sex trade. Another report estimated that 15% of the countries estimated two and one half million prostitutes are children. Many have been lured from their rural poverty into the sex trade.

The largest democracy in the world struggles to care for its masses under the weight of this poverty, illiteracy and ignorance. It is the earnest hope of the government of India to provide better opportunities for its people. One recently defined objective is to bring all the children under fourteen years old to school before 2010 A.D.

The plight of the women and children of India has not escaped the attention of Christians anxious to fulfill the holy mandate to care for the widows, orphans and homeless. Hundreds of small to medium size non government organizations have accepted the challenge and they need our help. How we can help is the purpose of this presentation.
